The Concepts I Implemented

My responsibility was to apply distributed software and system of systems (Sommerville Ch.17–19), making TANDIB work as a connected service across many devices and partners rather than a single isolated program.

How I implemented Distributed Software

  • I treated TANDIB as a distributed system: a central service that many separate devices reach over the network at the same time, not one program on one machine.
  • I handled the realities of distribution — devices and the central service communicate over the internet, so I designed for delays and for the connection sometimes failing.
  • I made sure information stays consistent across all those devices, so every user sees a correct and up-to-date picture.
  • This separation of concerns is what lets the system serve many users in different places simultaneously.

How I implemented System of Systems

  • I applied the idea that TANDIB is not one system but several independent systems working together as one experience.
  • Some of these systems are owned and operated by other parties, outside our control — our role was to safely orchestrate them, not to own them.
  • I designed clean boundaries between our system and the external ones, so a change or fault on their side does not break ours.
  • The result is a single, smooth service for the user, even though several independent systems stand behind it.

Reflection

I learned that modern software is rarely built alone — it lives in a network of devices and cooperates with systems owned by others. The hard part of distribution and systems-of-systems is not the happy case but the boundaries: what happens when the network is slow, or when a system we don't control behaves unexpectedly. Designing for those boundaries is what makes a distributed product actually work in the real world.
